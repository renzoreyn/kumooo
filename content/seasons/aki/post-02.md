# On editorial rhythm and edge caches

There's a boring truth about fast sites: most of the speed is caching, and most of the meaning is still prose. Kumooo renders in a Worker, caches aggressively, and gets out of the way. Aki is where you notice the "gets out of the way" part, because nothing in the chrome competes with a 1,200-word explanation of why your schema migration failed on a Tuesday.

Editorial rhythm is a set of small choices. Short sentences after long ones. A subheading when the topic shifts. A list when you have three concrete steps, not because lists test well in analytics, but because eyes latch onto them. I see engineers write beautiful commit messages and flat blog posts. Same facts. Different respect for the reader's time.

The ochre palette isn't nostalgia. It's contrast that stays warm under warm desk lamps, which is when a lot of us actually read. Dark mode cools the background without turning the page into a terminal. Links stay visible. Code blocks keep monospace clarity. You can paste a stack trace and it won't look like an afterthought.

When you pick aki over haru or natsu, you're saying your homepage is an index, not the main event. The main event is the article page. Seed four posts, write one real essay, and scroll. If you find yourself leaning forward, keep the theme. If you wish the home page shouted more, switch seasons. That's a feature, not a failure.
