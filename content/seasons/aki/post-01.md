# The chapter you skip is still part of the book

I used to chop every essay into tweet-sized chunks because I thought attention was gone. Turns out attention didn't leave. It just got picky.

Readers still finish long pieces when the type respects their eyes and the structure signals "this is worth staying for." Aki uses display serifs for titles and a calm sans for body copy so hierarchy reads before semantics. You feel the chapter break before you read the heading. That's not magic. It's spacing, weight, and a horizontal rule that doesn't scream for attention.

When I migrated my old blog off a bloated CMS, the hardest part wasn't export. It was admitting which posts were outline drafts pretending to be articles. Aki punishes that gently. Thin ideas look thin on cream paper. Good arguments get room to stack paragraphs until the point lands. I wrote this demo post with that in mind: one idea, several beats, no fake urgency.

If you're publishing technical work, treat each post like a paper section. State the problem in the opening graph. Build the middle with evidence. Close with what you'd tell a friend over coffee, not what you'd put on a billboard. The theme won't write for you, but it won't fight your sentences either.

Try reading this on a tablet in portrait mode. The measure stays readable. The drop cap on the first paragraph is decorative, not required. Disable it in your own CSS later if you want. For now, notice how it slows the eye at the start. Autumn is good for that kind of pause.
